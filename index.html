Choosing and Reflecting on Goals

K–2 Math

Supporting independent goal setting

Students are given a choice board with illustrated math goal options and student-friendly language. You read through the options together, and each student picks one that feels true for them right now. They record it by drawing or writing, then share it with a partner. Over time — across check-ins and brief conferences — students return to the goal and give examples of when they met it or name a strategy to get there. The goal isn't to get it right the first time; it's to build the habit of noticing and naming.

"Students were able to select a math goal more independently using the exemplars as a support. Some came up with their own goals outside of the ones I provided."

1st grade teacher

What makes it work

✓Illustrated options lower the barrier to entry. Students who can't yet read independently can still participate meaningfully.
✓Goal-setting can evolve across the year — what requires scaffolding in fall can become genuinely student-driven by spring. Let the process grow.
⚠Some students will pick randomly without connecting the goal to their actual learning. A quick partner share helps — if a student can't explain why they chose it, they probably need a different option.

3–4 Any subject

Clear or Cloudy reflection routine

After a lesson or learning task, students use a simple two-column organizer — one side for what feels clear, one for what feels cloudy. It takes about five minutes. The goal isn't to surface everything; it's to help students locate themselves in their learning right now, and then write one goal based on what's still cloudy. Used regularly across subjects, it becomes a low-stakes habit of self-checking. It connects naturally to POL competencies and 2nd Step goal-setting lessons.

What makes it work

✓The metaphor travels across subjects without needing new vocabulary. Students can use the same language in math, writing, and science.
✓Doing this regularly — not just once — is what builds the habit. A one-time activity is not the same as a routine.
⚠Goals written immediately after the reflection tend to stay vague. Build in one minute for students to share with a partner before writing — it sharpens what they name.

Self-Assessment + Conference

K–2 3–4 Writing

Scaffolded self reflection + conference

After students finish a processed piece of writing, they fill out a self-reflection sheet and sign up for a short conference. The self-reflection could be a checklist or a rubric — they mark places in their writing where they can show evidence of specific skills. During the conference, teacher and student look at the writing and the reflection together. If something is missing, you discuss it and set a goal. The student keeps the goal sheet in their writing folder; you keep a class copy to track at a glance.

"They liked being part of the conversation about their growth. It felt good to spend time with each student one on one about their learning."

3rd grade teacher

What makes it work

✓Having students find and mark evidence in their own writing before the conference makes the conversation faster and more productive. You're responding to their thinking, not leading it.
✓Keeping a class copy alongside the student copy gives you a bird's-eye view without adding tracking work between conferences.
⚠Some students will check everything off without actually looking at their writing. Ask them to point to a specific sentence that shows the skill before accepting any check.

5–6 Writing

Self-directed feedback review + conference

Give students time with the rubric and feedback on a previous piece before starting a new one. They choose one thing to work on and mark it on the rubric. They hold on to it while drafting — it becomes the anchor for a short conference to review and discuss the new piece. In the conference you follow their lead, giving feedback based on what they decided to work on.

"When students set a clear goal for their own writing, they wrote with more clarity and purpose — and demonstrated growth and pride because they own the process."

5th grade teacher

What makes it work

✓Feedback review happens before the new draft opens — not alongside it. Keeping those two moments separate matters.
✓One goal, not a list. When students try to work on everything, nothing actually shifts.
⚠Some students check things off without looking at their writing. Ask them to show you where in the piece they worked on their goal.

Analyze Exemplars to Co-Create Success Criteria

5–6 Writing

Writing exemplars and success criteria

Before writing, the class reads a strong example together and calls out what makes it work — clear ideas, strong evidence, voice, organization. You chart their thinking and help name those qualities as success criteria. Then students look at a developing example on the same topic and compare. Together they refine what quality looks like at different levels, and co-create a rubric from that conversation. Students use the rubric to plan, draft, revise, and give peer feedback. By the time they submit, they can explain how their writing meets the criteria — because they helped define them.

What makes it work

✓The co-creation step is what makes the rubric feel like theirs. A rubric handed to students is a checklist. A rubric they built is a standard they understand.
✓Using both a strong example and a developing one gives students the full range — not just a ceiling to aspire to, but a floor to push off from.
⚠This takes more up-front time than handing students a rubric. Plan for a full class session before writing begins. The payoff shows up in the quality of drafting and peer feedback.

POL Reflection + Goal Setting

3–4 POL / Any subject

Using POL progressions to make real-world connections

Students use POL progressions written in student-friendly language to locate where they are in a specific competency. They highlight their current level, set a goal, and identify small action steps — connecting their goal to what's already happening in class and in their daily lives outside school. Evidence gets collected across the trimester and organized in a binder or digital portfolio divided by competency.

Two things help make this real. First, you name and notice when competencies show up in the moment: "I noticed you were Adaptable when you worked to include others just now — I wonder how you might capture that in your binder." Second, students track everyday moments using a simple weekly planner, logging when they showed a competency at home or in the community and using AI to explore why it matters in the real world. This builds visibility: students stop seeing the competencies as abstract school values and start seeing them as things they're already doing and can keep growing in.

"Students have taken this on 100%. They have their goals open and are thinking about the actions they take daily that connect to their goal area. It's very hands-off from a teacher perspective."

4th grade teacher

What makes it work

✓Student-friendly progression language is the key ingredient. When students can read where they are, they can self-assess without you interpreting it for them.
✓Families responded strongly at conferences — not just to academic progress, but to seeing their child set goals and reflect as a lifelong skill.
⚠The binder system requires upfront setup and protected weekly time. Without consistent time to gather evidence and reflect, it becomes a collection folder rather than a living tool.

K–2 Any subject

Collaboration goals using a single-point rubric

Take one section of the collaboration rubric — just one — and use it as a single-point rubric for reflection after a collaborative task. Students look at the arrow scale (from "had trouble listening" to "listened to all speakers") and mark where their group was today. You debrief together: what did it look like when it went well? What got in the way? The goal isn't to grade the collaboration — it's to make the skill visible so students can name it, notice it, and set a specific next-step goal around it.

"Going slow and being more explicit in one area is powerful. It feels a lot more manageable to break this apart."

Kindergarten teacher

What makes it work

✓One section at a time is the move. Students understand a single-point rubric far better than the full rubric all at once — and can actually use it to reflect.
✓The arrow scale format gives students a concrete continuum instead of a vague label. They can place themselves on it without having to interpret levels.
⚠Without examples and non-examples beforehand, students self-rate inaccurately in both directions. Spend time discussing what each end of the scale actually looks like before asking them to use it.

Trimester Reflection

K–6 Any subject

Trimester reflection

At the end of a trimester, students take stock of how they're doing across academic areas and any other areas that matter to them. They name what they're genuinely proud of (glows), what they want to work on (grows), and write two goals with specific next steps for each. This can happen in slides — where each prompt gets its own page and students write or type their responses — or using a structured table that fits on one sheet.

However it's structured, the reflection becomes the anchor for parent conferences. The student's voice leads the conversation; parents receive the reflection to take home and continue the discussion. The teacher's role shifts from delivering a report to facilitating a conversation the student already started.

"The student's voice was heard in the conference. Parents felt their students had choice and a space to advocate for their own learning."

4th grade teacher

What makes it work

✓When students go into a conference with a reflection already in hand, the conversation is more productive from the start. They're sharing what they think — you're responding to it.
✓The next steps section is what separates a reflection from a goal. Build in time for students to write at least one specific action, not just a wish.
⚠Without a model, students write glows that are generic and grows that are vague. Show two or three strong examples before students start writing.
